Cities are not just where most people live; they are where most political questions get decided. These books approach urbanism historically, ecologically, and speculatively — asking what forms of social life cities have made possible, and what they might yet make possible.

The three books represent quite different registers of urban thinking. Mumford is a historian of civilisation, tracing the city from ancient Mesopotamia to the modern megalopolis with a sweeping, sometimes elegiac vision of what has been gained and lost. Bookchin is a theorist and polemicist, arguing that genuine ecological politics requires the reconstruction of municipal life on decentralised, democratic lines. Newitz is an archaeologist-journalist, using four vanished cities to ask why urban societies collapse — and what survivors carried forward. What connects them is a refusal to treat the city as a technical problem. It is, for each of them, a moral and political one.
