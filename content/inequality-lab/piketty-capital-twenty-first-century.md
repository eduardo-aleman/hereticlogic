Piketty's central claim is straightforward to state: when the rate of return on capital (r) exceeds the rate of economic growth (g), wealth concentrates. The formula r > g has become perhaps the most discussed inequality in contemporary political economy. Whether it deserves that status is another matter.

## What the Argument Does Well

The historical data assembled here is genuinely impressive. Piketty and his collaborators built the World Inequality Database from tax records, estate registries, and national accounts spanning two centuries and multiple countries. The empirical picture that emerges — a long 19th-century concentration, a mid-20th-century compression, and a late-20th-century return to divergence — is difficult to dispute at a descriptive level.

The book is also unusually honest about the political nature of economic arrangements. Piketty refuses the technocratic pretence that distribution is a residual of neutral market forces. Capital's share of income, he insists, is always partly the product of political choices about property rights, taxation, and labour law. This is correct and too rarely stated so plainly.

## Where the Argument Strains

The r > g mechanism is more suggestive than decisive. The rate of return on capital is not a single number; it varies dramatically by asset class, country, period, and (critically) risk. Piketty aggregates across these differences in ways that obscure as much as they reveal. A pension fund's real return is not the same as a dynastic real estate portfolio's, even if both technically count as "capital."

More seriously, the link between r > g as an accounting identity and r > g as a causal driver of concentration requires additional steps that Piketty does not always supply. High returns on capital are compatible with widely distributed capital ownership, as he himself acknowledges. What matters is who holds the capital — and that is a political and institutional question that the formula alone cannot answer.

The global wealth tax proposed in the final chapters is flagged as utopian even by the author. The analytical chapters and the normative chapters thus sit somewhat awkwardly together. The diagnosis is structural; the prescription requires a level of international political coordination that the book's own analysis gives no reason to expect.

## Verdict

A significant work of empirical political economy whose central theoretical mechanism is thinner than its reception suggested. The data changes what is possible to argue; the formula does not quite do the explanatory work assigned to it. Worth reading closely and critically — which is, in the end, the only way worth reading anything.
