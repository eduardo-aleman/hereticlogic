Piketty's central claim is straightforward to state: when the rate of return on capital (r) exceeds the rate of economic growth (g), wealth concentrates. The formula r > g has become perhaps the most discussed inequality in contemporary political economy. Whether it deserves that status is another matter. But the book's deeper contribution — one that its critics and enthusiasts alike tend to underplay — is the gap it opens between what is actually happening to wealth and the stories societies tell themselves about why it is happening.

## The Material Mechanism: Capital Eating Labour's Lunch

The empirical record Piketty assembles is hard to argue with at a descriptive level. Capital's share of national income fell dramatically through the mid-twentieth century — compressed by war, depression, progressive taxation, and strong labour organisation — and has been recovering steadily since the 1980s. This is not a marginal trend. In most rich economies, the top decile's share of wealth is back to levels last seen in the Belle Époque. The billionaire class is not an aberration produced by exceptional talent arriving at an exceptional moment. It is the predictable output of a system in which returns on existing assets compound faster than the economy as a whole grows.

The mechanism is not complicated. If you own capital — stocks, real estate, private equity, bonds — and that capital returns 4–5% annually in real terms while the economy grows at 1–2%, your share of total wealth grows automatically. You do not need to produce anything, innovate anything, or risk anything in any meaningful sense. You need to already own capital and to not spend all of your returns. Inheritance does the rest. Piketty's data show that inherited wealth, as a share of total wealth, has been rising for four decades and is on track to return to 19th-century levels within a generation or two.

What this produces structurally is a bifurcation: an economy in which labour income — wages, salaries, professional fees — is one thing, and capital income is another, and the two are on diverging trajectories. The working rich (high-earning professionals, managers, technologists) are prosperous but belong to a different economic universe than the capital-owning rich. And the merely-employed — the overwhelming majority of the population — are on a long, slow relative decline regardless of their productivity growth, educational attainment, or individual effort.

## The Ideological Cover: Meritocracy and Innovation

Here is where the book's implicit political argument becomes most interesting, even where Piketty himself is most reticent about stating it directly.

The concentration of capital income described above cannot legitimate itself by appealing to work, contribution, or desert in any straightforward sense. You cannot claim to have earned a return that compounds automatically from existing ownership. The ideological work therefore has to be done elsewhere — and in contemporary capitalism it is done primarily by two interlocking myths: **meritocracy** and **innovation**.

**Meritocracy** holds that economic outcomes reflect individual talent and effort. The rich are rich because they are smarter, more hardworking, more creative. Inequality is therefore not only tolerable but just: it is the market's signal to talent about where to deploy itself. This argument is not entirely without force as a description of some labour-market dynamics. But as an account of the billionaire class it is essentially fraudulent. The Forbes 400 is increasingly dominated by inheritance and by returns to existing capital rather than by the Schumpeterian entrepreneur building something from nothing. Even where founders do build genuine enterprises from scratch — and some do — the fortunes they accumulate bear no proportional relationship to their contribution relative to the employees, suppliers, and public infrastructure on which those enterprises depend. The meritocratic story mistakes the distribution of bargaining power for the distribution of contribution.

**Innovation** is the more sophisticated variant. Here the argument is not merely that the rich deserve their wealth but that their wealth-accumulation is socially functional: they are the risk-takers, the capital-allocators, the drivers of technological progress on which everyone's prosperity depends. Tax them or regulate them and you will kill the goose. This argument does somewhat better than meritocracy because it is at least making a consequentialist rather than a purely desert-based claim. But it survives scrutiny poorly. Most of the capital returns Piketty documents accrue not to venture-backed innovators but to real estate, financial assets, and established corporate equity. The innovation narrative is constructed from the most visible and sympathetic cases — the garage startup, the disruptive technology — and universalised to cover a capital class that is mostly doing nothing of the kind.

What meritocracy and innovation share is that they translate a structural outcome (capital outpacing labour) into a story about individual virtue and social function. They perform the ideological task of making an automatic mechanical process look like a just reward. Piketty does not use this language — he is a data economist, not a critical theorist — but his data makes the analysis available to anyone willing to push it further than he does.

## The Argument's Limits

The r > g mechanism is more suggestive than decisive. The rate of return on capital is not a single number; it varies dramatically by asset class, country, period, and risk. Piketty aggregates across these differences in ways that obscure as much as they reveal. A pension fund's real return is not the same as a dynastic real estate portfolio's, even if both count as "capital" in the accounting.

More seriously, r > g as a causal driver of concentration requires steps the formula alone cannot supply. High returns on capital are compatible with widely distributed capital ownership — as Piketty acknowledges. What concentrates wealth is not the return differential per se but the political and institutional arrangements that determine who holds capital in the first place, what can be done with it, and how it is taxed. Those arrangements are not exogenous to the dynamic Piketty describes; they are partly produced by the concentrated interests it generates. The book gestures at this feedback loop without fully theorising it.

The global wealth tax proposed in the final chapters is flagged by the author himself as utopian. The analysis chapters and the prescription chapters thus sit awkwardly together. The diagnosis is structural and compelling; the remedy requires a level of international political coordination that the book's own logic about the power of concentrated capital gives no reason to expect.

## Verdict

A landmark of empirical political economy whose deepest contribution is not the formula but the data that makes the ideological mystification of capital income visible. Meritocracy and innovation are not explanations of the billionaire class; they are cover stories for it. Piketty does not say this loudly enough, and his theoretical mechanism is thinner than its reception suggested. But the evidence he assembles makes the argument hard to avoid for anyone reading in good faith.
